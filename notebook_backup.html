Introduction#

Since the invention of the internet in 1983, the still relatively novel technology has been adopted widely in societies and people’s daily lives. It has been welcomed by open arms: people greatly enjoy using it as it provides many benefits such as online connectivity, widely accessible information, and the efficient access to services, just to name a few. So much so that many have started taken it for granted. It would be a cliché to say that the world has been globalizing for the past century, but the internet has only accelerated this process due to its interconnected nature. Those who have gained internet access have been able to benefit from the myriad advantages provided by both the technology and globalization. Economies, especially those in the Western hemisphere, have been able to thrive by leveraging the efficiency and productivity gained from widespread internet adoption. Conversely, those who have been unable to adopt this technology or have been excluded from the emerging digital economy have been deprived of its benefits. This effect is present both globally between countries, as well as domestically within societies. Scholars have coined this phenomenon of digital inequality the digital divide (Khiabany, 2003).

Dataset and preprocessing#

Dataset#

The World Development Indicators dataset is an elaborate collection of global data for a wide range of diverse indicators, covering socio-demographic, economic, political and environmental domains. It encompasses a total of 1442 features within these topics, such as political stability, GDP growth, school enrollment, unemployment rate, population and literacy rate. It includes information on 266 different countries within a time frame of 61 years, ranging from 1960 to 2021. The dataset provides a valuable resource for exploring global development patterns.

The dataset can be found on Kaggle: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/psycon/world-development-indicators?select=WDIData.csv

Preprocessing#

Due to the large size of the original dataset, two preprocessing steps were taken to reduce the size. First, the dataset was reduced to a smaller and more relevant time frame ranging from 1990 to 2020. Secondly, out of the 1442 columns only a certain amount of important features were selected to be included in the smaller dataset, dropping all unnecessary variables.

To support ease of use during analysis, the data was transformed into the wide format based on the columns ‘Country Name’, ‘Country Code’, ‘Indicator Name’ and ‘Indicator Code’, with every other variable in a separate column. In this way, the years were also converted into a column of ascending order, making the dataset easier to interpret. Finally, the remaining dataset was filtered, removing all duplicate country codes and filtering out any non-country data.

This resulted in a final dataset of workable size with a clear structure, only containing data of unique, individual countries.

Perspective 1: Digital divide and economic growth#

As the digital divide has developed over the years, we may firstly want to explore its relationship with the economy and possible links with economic growth or decline. We suspect that the digital divide increases economic inequality between countries, as countries with more access to digital technologies will have greater possibilities to expand economically compared to countries with less access.

GNI per capita is a commonly used indicator for a country’s economic resources, representing the total income generated by a country’s residents situated both nationally and abroad. An increase in GNI per capita over time may therefore illustrate financial and economic growth. The degree of a country’s internet access and usage may be well indicated by the percentage of the population using the internet. The digital divide can be illustrated through this measure by the gap that occurs between countries with high internet access and low internet access.
